# Platform Patterns And Source Levels

Use this before adapting an approved imitation draft to WeChat or Xiaohongshu.

## Source Levels

| Level | Source type | How to use |
|---|---|---|
| A | Original platform post/article with visible performance data | Strongest; can shape structure directly |
| B | Creator's own case report with performance claims | Useful, but mark as self-reported |
| C | Data project or operator analysis over many posts | Good for hypotheses; still not a raw corpus |
| D | SEO/agency article with generic advice | Weak; use only as provisional support |
| E | Memory or general platform lore | Do not use as rule |

If no A/B/C source exists for the platform and topic type, say the rule is unvalidated.

## Current Source Caveats

The first platform-pattern update found limited public raw examples:

- WeChat: accessible raw 10w+ article corpora are not generally exposed through public web search. One accessible WeChat-origin article was republished on Huxiu with self-reported "several articles reached tens of thousands of reads / thousands of shares"; this is Level B.
- Xiaohongshu: raw popular notes often require platform access and are not reliably crawlable. Public sources found are mainly data projects or operator拆解 articles claiming many notes analyzed; these are Level C/D depending on transparency.

Do not write "based on verified explosive posts" unless actual raw posts and metrics were reviewed.

## WeChat Pattern Rules

Use when adapting to WeChat:

1. **Not just a pasted long essay**: preserve depth, but add reader-navigation.
2. **First screen must carry conflict**: scene + question + why it matters.
3. **Use section blocks**: 2-4 blocks, each with a clear question or judgment. Small headings are acceptable when they improve scanning.
4. **Mobile readability**: short paragraphs, no citation scaffolding, no dense policy dump.
5. **Evidence as trust**: evidence should deepen credibility, not appear as links or footnotes.
6. **End with reflection or discussion**: avoid traffic-style "please like/share"; use a restrained question if interaction is needed.

Good WeChat adaptation should answer:

- Why should a professional reader spend 5-8 minutes here?
- What is the issue, what changed, what can be known, and what remains unresolved?
- Can a reader explain the article's core insight after reading only headings and anchor sentences?

## Xiaohongshu Pattern Rules

Use when adapting to Xiaohongshu:

1. **Design for cover first**: cover text should express one concrete question or contrast.
2. **Card script, not essay**: create 5-7 cards with one idea each.
3. **Scenario-led**: begin with a recognizable moment, not policy background.
4. **Save value**: give a checklist, distinction, or "what to look at next" that readers may save.
5. **Comment value**: close with a concrete experience question, not "what do you think?"
6. **No citation scaffolding**: sources stay in companion files.
7. **Avoid platform cheapening**: no exaggerated hook, fake persona, private-message bait, or medical advice.

Good Xiaohongshu adaptation should answer:

- What should be on each image/card?
- What is the one thing a user can save?
- What experience might a reader comment with?

## Adaptation Failure Modes

- WeChat and Xiaohongshu drafts are the same text with different line breaks.
- Xiaohongshu version has no cover/card logic.
- WeChat version has no reader-navigation or section logic.
- Platform rules are asserted without source level.
- "爆款" advice is copied even when it damages brand depth.
